The Calculus of Interruption

To be a valued partner, an AI must operate like a cognitive economist, continuously weighing the cost of a user's attention against the value of its information. This requires a dynamic, three-layered model.

1

Layer 1: Detect User's Cognitive State

The foundation is understanding the user's current mental state to estimate the "price" of an interruption. The AI must sense whether a user is focused, frustrated, or learning.

2

Layer 2: Score Information Salience

Next, the AI calculates the "value" of its potential contribution. Is the information urgent? Is it relevant to the user's current task? This score determines if an interruption is worth making.

3

Layer 3: Choose the Right Coordination Strategy

Finally, the AI synthesizes the "price" and "value" to select the optimal intervention method, from an immediate alert to scheduling it for later, ensuring the notification's intrusiveness matches its utility.

Immediate

For critical, high-urgency alerts only. (e.g., Security Warning)

Negotiated

Offer help but give the user control. (e.g., "Need a suggestion?")

Mediated

Pass to a notification manager for batching. (e.g., Low-priority updates)

Scheduled

Hold until a natural breakpoint in activity. (e.g., "FYI, your doc was updated.")

The Art of Conversational Repair

Breakdowns in communication are inevitable. A truly intelligent AI is defined by its ability to gracefully recover from misunderstanding, turning failure into a collaborative success.

The Four Types of Repair

Self-Initiated Self-Repair

"I need to go to the... I mean, the post office."

Other-Initiated Self-Repair

A: "See you Tuesday." B: "Tuesday?" A: "Sorry, Wednesday."

Self-Initiated Other-Repair

"What's the name of that actor...?"

Other-Initiated Other-Repair

A: "He's from Boston." B: "Actually, Chicago."

Human conversation shows a strong preference for self-repair. Instead of saying "I don't understand," a good AI should prompt the user to clarify, empowering them to fix the issue.

Principles for a Human-Centered AI

To avoid the pitfalls of the past (like Clippy), the next generation of AI must be built on a foundation of deference, transparency, and ethical design.

The Clippy Anti-Patterns 👎

  • Context-blind and not personalized
  • Invasive and ignorant of user state
  • Optimized for novelty, not utility
  • Insular design process ignoring feedback
  • Technologically over-ambitious for its time

The HCAI Principles 👍

  • Deference: Honor user autonomy
  • Pertinence: Be valuable and relevant
  • Flexibility: Adapt to any context
  • Learned Adaptation: Improve from every interaction
  • Augment, Don't Replace: Enhance human capability

A Proactive Ethical Framework

🔍

Inferential Transparency

Users can ask "Why did you do that?" and get a clear answer.

🤝

Dynamic Consent

Seek "micro-consent" for actions rather than relying on a one-time agreement.

⚙️

User Auditing & Control

Allow users to review and correct the AI's inferences about them.

🛡️

Context-Aware Constraints

Automatically limit data collection during privacy-sensitive tasks.
